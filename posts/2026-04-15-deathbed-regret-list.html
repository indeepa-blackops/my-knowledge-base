The Deathbed Regret List: How to Write Your Own Ending

In 1888, Alfred Nobel opened a newspaper to find his own obituary. It was a mistake—his brother Ludvig had passed away—but the headline "The Merchant of Death is Dead" shook him to his core. Horrified by how the world saw him, Nobel spent the rest of his life rewriting his legacy, eventually founding the Nobel Prizes.

Most of us won’t get a "premature obituary" to serve as a wake-up call. However, we can use the Deathbed Regret List—a framework popularized by Sahil Bloom—to project ourselves to the end of our lives and identify what we would regret not doing. Use inverse thinking to act now, so you don't have to wonder "what if" later.

1

The Pillars of Vitality

Regret often stems from the things we didn't do for ourselves because they felt too difficult or uncomfortable in the moment.

Protect Your "House"

Your body and mind are the only places you have to live. Letting them deteriorate isn't just a personal loss; it impacts everyone who relies on you. Investing in your health daily is a non-negotiable insurance policy for your future.

The Courage to Leap

We spend years waiting for "permission" to start a business, change careers, or take a risk. In reality, permission isn't granted—it’s taken. Don't let fear of failure rob you of the attempt.

Seek Challenging Quests

Easy comfort is a slow poison. Hard-earned progress and the struggle for growth are what give life a sense of meaning. Look for the "uphill" path; it usually has the best view.

2

The Social Fabric

On a deathbed, no one wishes they had spent more time at the office. They wish they had spent more time with the people who mattered.

The "Magic Years"

Statistically, 95% of the total time you will ever spend with your children happens before they turn 18. If you have kids in the house, you are currently in the "Magic Years." Be present before they become a memory.

Proximity to Parents

Quality of life is often dictated by the proximity of those we love. If you haven't seen your parents enough, consider how proximity—not just occasional visits—can fundamentally change the relationship.

The Harvard Study Insight

An 80-year-long study found that the #1 predictor of health and happiness at age 80 wasn't wealth or fame—it was the quality of your relationships. Do not let your deepest connections atrophy over time.

3

The Presence Paradox

The final category of regrets involves the modern distractions that pull us away from a grounded, intentional life.

Financial Security

Leaving a family in financial ruin is a heavy weight to carry. Building an emergency fund isn't about greed; it’s about providing a "strong foundation" that allows you to focus on everything else with peace of mind.

Real Connection > Digital

Technology is a master at destroying presence. Put the phone down. A real connection in the physical world—looking someone in the eyes—is infinitely more valuable than the endless scroll of digital noise.

Finding Your "Enough"

Ambition is a natural drive, but it can easily become a treadmill with no end. If you lose sight of what is "enough," you will spend your entire life chasing a finish line that simply doesn't exist.

The Bottom Line

You don't get to choose when your story ends, but you are the only one holding the pen.

The Deathbed Regret List isn't meant to be morbid; it’s meant to be a catalyst. You won’t get to read your obituary—but you can start writing your ending today.
