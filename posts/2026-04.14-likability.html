Why some people are instantly likable

By Eleanor Vance • 5 min read

We've all met them. The person who walks into a room and, within five minutes, has everyone at ease. They aren't always the loudest, the funniest, or the most attractive. Yet, people naturally gravitate toward them.

For a long time, psychologists believed likability was a fixed trait—you were either born with undeniable charisma or doomed to be socially awkward. But recent research suggests a different reality: likability isn't a personality trait; it's a set of subtle signals.

The problem with "playing it cool"

When entering a new social situation, most of us default to a defensive posture. We want to be liked, but we are terrified of being rejected. So, we play it safe. We wait for others to approach us. We give polite, short answers. We try to appear independent, unimpressed, or "cool."

This strategy backfires entirely.

💡 The Liking Gap

Studies show that after a conversation, people consistently underestimate how much the other person liked them. We assume others are judging us harshly, when in reality, they are usually focused on their own insecurities. This "liking gap" makes us hold back exactly when we should be leaning in.

The warmth and competence equation

Princeton psychologist Susan Fiske discovered that when we meet someone new, our brains rapidly assess them on two main criteria: warmth (Are they friendly? Do they have good intentions?) and competence (Are they capable? Can they act on those intentions?).

While many ambitious professionals focus entirely on proving their competence, research shows that warmth is the primary factor in likability. If someone perceives you as competent but cold, they might respect you, but they will also view you as a threat. Warmth must always precede competence.

"A person who projects warmth makes others feel safe, seen, and valued. That feeling is the foundation of all charisma."

Two practical ways to signal warmth

You don't need to change your entire personality to project warmth. You just need to change the signals you are broadcasting.

1. Assume they already like you

This is the ultimate social hack. Before walking into a party or a meeting, tell yourself: "These people already like me." This mental shift automatically changes your body language. You will stand taller, smile more easily, make better eye contact, and speak with a relaxed tone. By acting as if you are already friends, you give them the psychological safety to be friendly back.

2. Ask for advice, not just information

Asking someone "What time is the meeting?" gets you data. Asking someone "How do you usually prepare for these meetings?" gets you a connection. Asking for advice shows humility, elevates the other person's status, and signals that you value their opinion. It turns a transaction into an interaction.

The real takeaway: drop the armor first

Most social anxiety is protective. We guard ourselves against rejection by seeming cool, detached, or hard to read. The problem is that this armor achieves exactly what we're trying to avoid — it keeps people at a distance, and then we conclude that distance is inevitable.

The research suggests something more hopeful: likability is less about who you are and more about what you signal. And the signal that matters most — warmth — is something you can choose to send, in any room, at any moment.

Go first. Make it safe for others. The cycle will follow.
